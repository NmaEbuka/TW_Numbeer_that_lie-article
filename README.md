## Objective:
The goal of this article is to demonstrate how commonly used metrics like averages, percentages, and trends can distort reality when presented without context, and to provide practical ways to interpret them correctly in business settings.

## Audience:
This work is designed for:
- Business decision-makers (managers, founders, executives)
- Aspiring and early-stage data analysts
- Professionals who rely on reports and dashboards for decision-making
Anyone who works with data but may not always question how it is presented will benefit from this.

## Thought Process:
I was reading a book on data presentation, and it was talking about how if the results of the analysis are not presented well, the entire purpose of the analysis will be missed.
So, I started doing research about common ways numbers can be misleading. Most times, it is not intentional.
I didn't want the article to be too technical. I wanted it in simple terms and used real-world business scenarios to explain each concept and how they impact real decisions, money and business strategy.

## Structure
- The Average That Hides Everything
  - **Problem:** How outliers can affect averages and make them not representative of the whole distribution. I added a picture of a sample business data to demonstrate.
  - **What to do:** Ask for distribution behind the average. Use medians
- The Percentage That Sounds Impressive
  - **Problem:** Percentages without base values are incomplete and are good at making large risks look manageable. Also included a picture of a sample data.
  - **What to do:** Percentages should be included with absolute figures.
- The Chosen Time Frame that Wins an Argument
  - **Problem:** How selective time ranges can create misleading trends. How a particular period is presented to either look good or make a situation look bad.
  - **What to do**: Stanardise time frame across reporting.
- Correlation Presented as Causation
  - **Problem:** How relationships in data do not always imply cause
  - **What to do:** Treat correlation as the basis for further investigation.
- The Sample Size Behind the Headline
  - **Problem:** How small or biased samples affect reliability
  - **What to do:** When presenting findings, state the sample size.
- Conclusion: The Bigger Picture
  The analyst is not being dishonest. The figures might be mathematically correct, but not suitable for the decisions that need to be made.
